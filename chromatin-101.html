A primer from the lab

Chromatin, from the ground up

Two meters of DNA fold into a nucleus a few microns wide — and how that folding is marked, read, and remembered is the heart of what we study. This is a short, friendly tour of the ideas behind the research.

≈ 6 minute read · no background assumed
01 — 一

A packaging problem

Every one of your cells holds about two meters of DNA inside a nucleus only a few millionths of a meter across. To fit — and to stay usable — that DNA can't just be crammed in. It is wound, folded, and organized into a material called chromatin. How tightly a stretch of DNA is packaged decides whether its genes can be switched on or kept silent.

02 — 二

The nucleosome: beads on a string

The basic unit is the nucleosome: about 147 base pairs of DNA wound roughly twice around a spool of eight histone proteins. Strung together, nucleosomes look like beads on a string. This is already a huge compaction — but it's also the layer where regulation happens.

03 — 三

Marks on the tails

Histones have flexible tails that stick out, and cells decorate them with small chemical tags — methyl groups, acetyl groups, and more. These histone modifications don't change the DNA sequence at all; they change how the DNA is read.

One mark our lab studies closely is H3K9 methylation (a methyl tag on the 9th amino acid of histone H3) — a hallmark of silenced chromatin.

04 — 四

Two states: open and closed

Marks push chromatin toward one of two broad states. Euchromatin is loose and accessible — genes here can be transcribed. Heterochromatin is densely packed and silent — the machinery can't get in. The same DNA can sit in either state, and switching between them is how a cell turns programs on and off without editing a single letter.

05 — 五

Writers, readers, erasers

Three kinds of proteins manage the marks. Writers add them, readers recognize them and recruit more machinery, and erasers remove them. In fission yeast, the writer Clr4 deposits H3K9 methylation, and the reader Swi6/HP1 binds that mark to compact chromatin and spread silencing.

Crucially, some readers also recruit writers — a feedback loop that lets a mark copy itself to its neighbors.

06 — 六

Memory: copied across divisions

When a cell divides, its DNA is copied — but the histone marks must be copied too, or the memory of which genes were silent would be lost. Thanks to the reader-recruits-writer feedback, a daughter cell can re-establish the parent's pattern of marks. This is epigenetic inheritance: heritable information carried on top of the genetic sequence.

Where the research begins

Every question in our lab lives somewhere in this picture — how a mark is established, how it is maintained, and how it is inherited.
